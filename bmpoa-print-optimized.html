Blue Mountain Sanitary District

Understanding Our Legal Structure

Blue Mountain Property Owners Association (BMPOA) operates as a Sanitary District under Warren County regulations. This unique legal status enables us to provide essential services to our mountain community while maintaining local control and governance.

🏛️
What is a Sanitary District?

A Sanitary District is a special administrative unit established under Virginia law to provide essential services such as water, sewer, and waste management to a defined geographic area. It operates with specific powers granted by the state and county.

🛠️
Services We Provide
  • Road maintenance and snow removal
  • Fire mitigation and wood chipping
  • Common area maintenance
  • Covenant enforcement
  • Community facilities management
  • Emergency coordination

Benefits of Our Sanitary District Status

✓
Legal Authority

Ability to levy assessments and enforce regulations under state law

✓
Tax Benefits

Property owners qualify for certain tax advantages and deductions

✓
Local Control

Community-elected board maintains direct oversight of services

✓
Cost Efficiency

Shared services reduce individual property owner costs

📍 Important for Property Owners

All property within the Blue Mountain Sanitary District boundaries is subject to annual assessments and must comply with district regulations. These assessments fund the essential services that maintain our community's infrastructure and quality of life. Property owners automatically become members of BMPOA upon purchasing property within the district.

III. Community Regulations

Blue Mountain's regulations are designed to maintain property values, ensure safety, and preserve the natural beauty of our community. All property owners are bound by the restrictive covenants recorded with their deeds.

Building & Construction

All new construction, additions, and major renovations require approval from the Architectural Review Committee (ARC) before work begins.

ARC Review Process

  1. Submit Plans: Provide detailed plans including site plan, elevations, and materials list
  2. Committee Review: ARC meets monthly to review submissions
  3. Site Visit: Committee members may visit your property
  4. Decision: Approval, conditional approval, or denial within 30 days
  5. Appeals: Decisions may be appealed to the Board of Directors

Design Standards

Element Requirement
Minimum Square Footage 1,200 sq ft (single story), 1,500 sq ft (multi-story)
Setbacks Front: 35 ft, Side: 15 ft, Rear: 25 ft
Height Limit 35 feet from average grade
Exterior Colors Earth tones that blend with natural surroundings
Roofing Architectural shingles or metal in approved colors
Foundations Must be faced with stone, brick, or approved siding

Construction Hours

Construction work is permitted Monday through Friday 7:00 AM to 6:00 PM, Saturday 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM. No construction on Sundays or federal holidays without special permission.

Property Use Guidelines

Permitted Uses

  • Single-family residential dwellings
  • Home offices that don't generate customer traffic
  • Detached garages and storage buildings (with ARC approval)

  • Swimming pools and hot tubs (with proper screening)

Prohibited Uses

  • Commercial businesses with customer traffic
  • Multi-family dwellings or apartments

  • Livestock or poultry (except as noted below)

Pet Regulations

  • Dogs and cats must be kept under owner's control
  • Limit of 4 dogs per household
  • No commercial breeding operations
  • Horses permitted on lots of 3+ acres with proper fencing
  • Chickens allowed (up to 6 hens, no roosters) with neighbor consent

Landscaping and Trees

Property owners are encouraged to maintain the natural beauty of their lots while creating defensible space for fire safety.

  • Tree Removal: Clearing more than 1/4 acre requires ARC approval
  • Native Plants: Use of native species is strongly encouraged
  • Erosion Control: Required on slopes greater than 20%
  • Outdoor Lighting: Must be shielded to prevent light pollution

Chapter III: Regulations 6

Vehicle & Parking Rules

General Vehicle Regulations

  • All vehicles must be currently registered and operable
  • Speed limit on all Blue Mountain roads: 20 MPH
  • Yield to pedestrians and wildlife at all times
  • No parking on roads or shoulders except for emergencies

Recreational Vehicles

Permitted

  • RVs stored on owner's property
  • Boats on trailers
  • ATVs for property maintenance
  • Guest RVs (up to 14 days)

Restrictions

  • Must be screened from road view
  • No permanent RV living
  • No RV rentals permitted
  • ATVs not for recreation on roads

Commercial Vehicles

Commercial vehicles are restricted to preserve the residential character of the community:

  • No vehicles over 10,000 lbs GVW without approval
  • No commercial logos larger than 2 sq ft
  • Must be parked in garages or screened from view
  • Temporary exemptions for construction/delivery vehicles

Winter Driving Safety

Blue Mountain roads can be challenging in winter conditions:

  • 4WD or AWD strongly recommended
  • Carry chains November through March
  • Keep emergency supplies in vehicle
  • Check road conditions before traveling

Enforcement

The Board of Directors enforces vehicle regulations through:

  1. Warning Notice: First violation
  2. Fine: $50 for second violation
  3. Increased Fines: $100+ for repeated violations
  4. Legal Action: For persistent violators

All fines collected are deposited in the community's general fund for road maintenance.

Road Maintenance & Winter Weather

Important: No Salt on Gravel Roads

Salt use is prohibited on BMPOA gravel roads. Salt damages the roadbed and significantly increases maintenance costs. Blue barrels of gravel chips are placed on steep hills for traction during winter weather.

BMPOA Road System

The Blue Mountain Property Owners Association maintains approximately 15 miles of private roads throughout our community. These roads are funded through the Sanitary District tax assessment and maintained by the Roads Committee.

Winter Weather Operations

Service Details
Snow Plowing Trigger Begins when 4 inches of snow accumulates at the designated mountain location
Priority Routes Main access roads cleared first, followed by secondary roads
Traction Materials Blue barrels with gravel chips located on steep grades
Resident Responsibility Clear driveways after plow passes; do not push snow into roads

Winter Driving Recommendations

Vehicle Preparation

  • Tire Chains: Recommended for ice/snow conditions
  • Practice Installation: Learn before winter arrives
  • 4WD/AWD: Helpful but not a substitute for chains
  • Emergency Kit: Include shovel, sand/kitty litter, flashlight

Driving Tips

  • Reduce speed significantly on snow/ice
  • Allow extra distance for stopping
  • Avoid sudden acceleration or braking
  • Know alternate routes in case of blockage

Emergency Situations

Downed Trees on Roads

Residents may safely clear small branches and debris. For large trees:

  • Report location via "Contact Us" on BMPOA.org
  • Include exact location and photos if possible
  • Do not attempt to move trees near power lines

Fallen Power Lines - EXTREME DANGER

  • If a line falls on your vehicle: STAY INSIDE the vehicle
  • If you must exit: Jump clear without touching the car and ground simultaneously
  • Shuffle away: Keep feet together, shuffle at least 35 feet away
  • Report immediately: Call Rappahannock Electric Cooperative (REC) at (800) 552-3904
  • Online reporting: myrec.coop/outagecenter

Wood-Chipping Community Effort

All property owners within the BMPOA Sanitary District are eligible for free wood chipping annually to reduce fire risk. This valuable service helps reduce fire hazards around homes while providing a convenient way to dispose of woody debris. The program is a critical component of our community's wildfire prevention strategy.

Surplus wood chips are delivered free of charge to homeowner property during chipping week. Extra chips are dumped at The Lodge and Deer Lake dam parking areas for pickup. Chipping typically occurs early spring (duration ~1 week), announced weeks in advance via BMPOA.org, newsletter, and Facebook group.

Program Benefits

  • Fire Prevention: Reduces fuel loads around homes
  • Free Service: No cost to property owners in good standing
  • Convenience: Curbside pickup of prepared brush piles
  • Free Wood Chips: Available for landscaping and erosion control
  • Community Safety: Creates defensible space throughout Blue Mountain

Partnership with Virginia Department of Forestry

The wood-chipping program is conducted in partnership with the Virginia Department of Forestry as part of their wildfire prevention efforts. This collaboration recognizes Blue Mountain as a Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) area where homes are interspersed with natural vegetation that can fuel wildfires.

Benefits of this partnership include:

  • Access to professional equipment and expertise
  • Cost-sharing that makes the program financially sustainable
  • Educational resources on creating defensible space
  • Follow-up assessments and recommendations
  • Integration with county-wide fire prevention efforts

Professional wood chipping equipment processing brush piles during the annual community fire prevention program

Brush Pile Preparation Guidelines

Proper preparation of your brush pile is essential for efficient chipping. Follow these guidelines carefully:

Requirement Specification Reason
Location Within 5 feet of roadside Allows equipment access
Branch Diameter Maximum 8 inches Chipper capacity limit
Branch Length Maximum 12 feet Handling safety
Pile Height Under 4 feet Stability and safety
Orientation Cut ends facing road Efficient feeding into chipper
Separation Vines/thorny plants separate Different processing needs

Materials NOT Accepted

  • Lumber, treated wood, or construction debris
  • Root balls with dirt and rocks
  • Stumps and chunks of wood over 8" diameter
  • Grass clippings, leaves, or garden waste
  • Household trash or non-vegetation items
  • Wire, nails, rope, or other non-wood materials

Free Wood Chip Delivery & Access

The wood-chipping program generates large quantities of wood chips that are available to Blue Mountain residents at no charge. These chips are valuable resources for landscaping and fire prevention.

Direct Delivery During Chipping Week

When the chipping crew processes your brush pile, you can request that they leave the resulting chips on your property rather than hauling them away. This provides fresh chips directly to your home.

To arrange delivery: Contact J Cook at jcook0313@gmail.com

Year-Round Pickup Locations

Wood chips are available at these communal stockpile locations:

  • Lodge Parking Area: 540 Cliff Road (near The Blue Mountain Lodge)
  • Deer Lake Dam: Parking area near the dam

First-come, first-served basis. Bring your own containers/vehicle for loading.

Eligibility & Enrollment

No pre-registration required. All properties within the BMPOA Sanitary District boundaries automatically qualify for this service. Simply prepare your brush pile according to the guidelines and it will be chipped during the scheduled week.

Scheduling & Notification

  • Annual Schedule: Typically occurs in early spring (April/May)
  • Duration: Approximately one week for the entire community
  • Notification: Announced weeks in advance via:
    • BMPOA.org website

If you miss the annual chipping or have materials that don't qualify:

  • Composting: Create brush piles in back areas for natural decomposition
  • Professional Services: Hire private tree services for large-scale clearing
  • Transfer Station: Bentonville Transfer Station accepts woody waste (fees apply)

Wildfire Safety & Defensible Space

Wildfire is the most significant natural threat to Blue Mountain. Every property owner must take steps to protect their home and family through proper preparation and maintenance of defensible space.

Creating Defensible Space

Zone 1: Immediate Zone (0-5 feet)

Goal: Create a non-combustible area around your home

  • Hardscape only: Use gravel, stone pavers, or concrete
  • No combustibles: Remove all flammable materials including mulch
  • Clean zones: Keep areas under decks and porches clear
  • Screen vents: Install 1/16" metal mesh on all vents
  • Seal gaps: Close openings where embers could enter

Zone 2: Intermediate Zone (5-30 feet)

Goal: Create breaks in vegetation to slow fire spread

  • Careful landscaping: Use low-growing, low-flammability plants
  • Lawn areas: Keep grass mowed to maximum 4 inches
  • Tree canopies: Prune up 6-10 feet (or 1/3 tree height)
  • Spacing: 10+ feet between tree crowns, 3x shrub height between shrubs
  • Remove: Dead vegetation, leaf litter, pine needles regularly
  • Firewood: Store at least 30 feet from structures
  • Propane tanks: Clear 10-foot radius of vegetation

Zone 3: Extended Zone (30-100 feet)

Goal: Interrupt fire path and reduce flame lengths

  • Tree spacing: 12+ feet between canopies on flat ground (more on slopes)
  • Remove ladder fuels: Clear vegetation under trees
  • Thin forest: Remove small trees and brush between mature trees
  • Create islands: Group plants in well-spaced clusters
  • Maintain access: Keep driveways clear for fire equipment
  • Natural fuel breaks: Use rock outcroppings, roads, streams

Zone 4: Beyond 100 feet

  • Thin trees to healthy spacing
  • Remove dead trees and fallen logs
  • Maintain access roads for fire equipment
  • Consider creating firebreaks on steep slopes

Home Hardening Checklist

  • ☐ Install metal or Class A fire-rated roofing
  • ☐ Cover vents with 1/8-inch metal mesh
  • ☐ Install dual-pane windows with tempered glass
  • ☐ Use non-combustible siding materials
  • ☐ Enclose areas under decks and porches
  • ☐ Clear debris from gutters monthly

Bear Safety on the Mountain

Black bears are intelligent, typically non-aggressive animals that avoid humans. Most conflicts occur because bears seek food attractants, not out of aggression. As Blue Mountain residents, we must be responsible stewards by removing attractants and understanding bear behavior.

🐻 Remember: A fed bear is a dead bear. Human food sources lead to aggressive behavior and eventual euthanization.

🐻
Virginia Population
18,000-20,000

Bear-Proofing Your Property

⚠️ REMOVE THESE ATTRACTANTS TO PREVENT BEAR VISITS ⚠️

Food Sources
  • Bird feeders - Remove April 1 - November 30
  • Pet food - Never leave outdoors
  • Livestock feed - Store in bear-proof containers
  • Fruit trees - Harvest ripe fruit immediately
  • Gardens - Install electric fencing
  • Beehives - Protect with electric fence
Waste & Storage
  • Garbage - Store in locked building until pickup
  • Recycling - Rinse all containers thoroughly
  • Compost - No meat, bones, or grease
  • Grills - Clean after each use, burn off grease
  • Coolers - Never leave on porches/decks
  • Scented items - Store toiletries indoors

HIGH ACTIVITY

  • Remove feeders by April 1
  • Secure all garbage
  • Check property daily

☀️ SUMMER

June - August
MODERATE

Cubs active. Natural foods available.

  • Clean grills immediately
  • Pick ripe fruit daily
  • Secure pet food

Sept - November
EXTREME RISK

Hyperphagia: Bears eat 20,000 cal/day!

  • Double-check everything
  • Bears desperate for food

LOW ACTIVITY

Most bears denning. Some remain active.

  • Continue securing trash
  • Bird feeders safer now

Hazing Techniques & Community Cooperation

Hazing teaches bears to fear humans and avoid properties. Community-wide participation is essential for success.

✓ Approved Methods

  • Noise: Air horns, pots/pans, yelling
  • Projectiles: Paintballs at rump, rubber slugs
  • Water: Garden hose, sprinklers
  • Motion devices: Activated alarms/lights
  • Visual: Opening/closing umbrellas

⚠️ Safety Rules

  • NEVER corner a bear
  • Maintain 50+ ft distance
  • Have escape route ready
  • Don't haze cubs or mothers
  • Stop if bear approaches

Bear Encounter Protocols

🐻 CRITICAL ENCOUNTER GUIDELINES 🐻

✓ DO
  • Remain calm and assess situation
  • Back away slowly, facing bear
  • Speak in calm, low voice
  • Make yourself appear larger
  • Give bear escape route
  • If indoors, open doors/windows
  • Use bear spray if <50 feet
  • Fight back if attacked
✗ DON'T
  • Run (triggers chase instinct)
  • Climb trees (bears climb better)
  • Make direct eye contact
  • Turn your back
  • Scream or make sudden moves
  • Corner or trap the bear
  • Get between cubs and mother
  • Play dead with black bears
Emergency Contact: Wildlife Conflict Hotline: (804) 367-1000
For immediate danger, call 911 first

Bear Facts & Myth Busting

🐻 Understanding Black Bear Behavior

❌ MYTHS
  • Bears standing = aggression
    (They stand to see/smell better)
  • Mother bears always attack
    (Usually flee if given space)
  • Bears are strictly nocturnal
    (Active dawn/dusk but anytime)
  • Relocating solves problems
    (New bears move in)
✓ FACTS
  • Diet is ~80% plant-based
  • Can run 35 mph
  • Excellent climbers & swimmers
  • Sense of smell 7x better than dogs
  • Can detect food 5+ miles away
  • Remember food sources for years

History and Founding Principles

The Birth of Blue Mountain

Created in the late 1950s as a vacation community for Washington D.C. residents, Blue Mountain was envisioned as a peaceful mountain retreat where city dwellers could escape to clean mountain air and natural beauty. The founders carefully designed the subdivision to preserve the mountain's character while providing modern amenities.

Evolution of Our Community

What began as a weekend and summer retreat has evolved into a vibrant mix of seasonal residents and year-round families. The transition reflects changing lifestyles and the appeal of mountain living, with many former vacation homes now serving as primary residences.

Founding Principles That Guide Us Today

  • Preserve Natural Beauty: Maintain the mountain's pristine environment
  • Foster Community: Create connections among neighbors
  • Provide Essential Services: Ensure quality infrastructure and amenities
  • Respect Privacy: Balance community needs with individual property rights
  • Sustainable Development: Growth that respects our mountain heritage

The Sanitary District

A pivotal moment in Blue Mountain's history was the establishment of the Sanitary District. This special designation ensures sustainable funding for road maintenance and community services through property tax assessments, providing stability and fairness in sharing community costs.

BMPOA Leadership

The Blue Mountain Property Owners Association has been managed by an elected executive board since its inception. This volunteer leadership model ensures that those making decisions about our community are invested property owners who understand and share our values.

Key Milestones

  • Late 1950s: Blue Mountain subdivision established
  • Early Years: Infrastructure development, road construction
  • Sanitary District: Created to ensure sustainable funding
  • The Lodge: Community center established for gatherings
  • Deer Lake: Recreation area developed for members
  • Today: Thriving community of 300+ properties

Our Mountain Heritage

Blue Mountain's location in the Blue Ridge Mountains connects us to centuries of Appalachian history. From Native American hunting grounds to colonial settlements, our mountain has witnessed the evolution of American frontier life. Today, we continue this legacy as stewards of this special place.

  • Surface: 2 inches crusher run
  • Width: 10 feet between ditches
  • Grading: Proper crowning for drainage
  • Culverts: Required at all low points

Once inspected and accepted by BMPOA Roads Committee, maintenance becomes BMPOA responsibility.

Fence Requirements

Only split-rail fences are allowed.

  • Submit design and materials plan to ARC
  • Written approval required before installation
  • Exceptions considered case-by-case based on compatibility

Construction Timeline

  • Construction may not begin until written ARC approval received
  • Any deviation from approved plans risks legal action
  • All exterior work must be completed within one year of start

Compliance & Enforcement

Failure to Comply Process:

  1. Written Notice: BMPOA notifies owner of violation
  2. 60 Days: To correct violation, OR
  3. 20 Days: To submit alternative plan/timetable
  4. ARC Review: 30 days to approve/deny alternative
  5. Appeals: To Arbitration Committee within 30 days
  6. Legal Action: If not resolved, Board may pursue

Blue Mountain's natural beauty is our greatest asset. From spring wildflowers to autumn foliage, our mountain ecosystem provides year-round wonder. This chapter helps you appreciate and protect our natural heritage.

Protecting Our Trilliums

🌸 Blue Mountain's Spring Spectacular

Each spring, nearby Thompson Wildlife Management Area hosts one of nature's most breathtaking displays — 18 million Great White Trilliums carpet the forest floor, creating a sea of white blooms visible from space!

The breathtaking trillium bloom at Thompson WMA - one of nature's most spectacular displays

🏵️
Peak Bloom
Late April - Early May

Understanding Trilliums

🌱 Life Cycle Facts

  • 9+ years from seed to first bloom
  • 25+ years lifespan per plant
  • Spring ephemeral - blooms before trees leaf out
  • Ant-dispersed seeds (myrmecochory)
  • Deep roots make transplanting fatal
  • Single stem - damage kills entire plant

📍 Species at Thompson WMA

  • Great White Trillium (T. grandiflorum)
  • Red Trillium (T. erectum)
  • Painted Trillium (T. undulatum)
  • Large-flowered Trillium (T. grandiflorum)
  • Plus 15+ other spring wildflowers!

⚠️ CRITICAL CONSERVATION RULES ⚠️

🚫 NEVER

  • Pick flowers (kills the plant)
  • Dig up or transplant
  • Walk off established trails
  • Mow until foliage yellows (June)
  • Use herbicides nearby

✓ ALWAYS

  • Stay on marked trails
  • Take only photographs
  • Report illegal picking
  • Educate visitors
  • Support conservation efforts

Violators face fines under Virginia's Native Plant Protection Act

Visiting Thompson Wildlife Management Area

📍 Location & Access

  • Entry: Via Freezeland Road, Linden
  • Distance: ~10 minutes from Blue Mountain
  • Parking: Limited spaces at trailhead
  • Best Time: 8-10 AM (fewer crowds)
  • Peak Bloom: Last week April - First week May

🥾 Trillium Trail Loop

  • Length: ~1 mile loop
  • Difficulty: Easy to moderate
  • Features: Interpretive signs
  • Connects to: Appalachian Trail
  • Wildlife: Deer, turkey, songbirds

🌺 Other Spring Wildflowers at Thompson WMA

Virginia Bluebells • Bloodroot • Dutchman's Breeches • Jack-in-the-Pulpit • Wild Ginger • Spring Beauty • Hepatica • Trout Lily • Mayapple • Wild Geranium • Solomon's Seal • False Solomon's Seal • Foam Flower • Wood Anemone • Toothwort

🌸 Be a Trillium Guardian 🌸

When you see trilliums on your property, celebrate! You're hosting ancient plants that witnessed the mountains' formation. Mark their locations, protect them from mowing, and share their beauty through photos — never picking.

Fire-Resistant Native Landscaping

Choose native plants that are also fire-resistant for defensible space zones:

Low Flammability Natives

  • Groundcovers: Wild ginger, Green-and-gold
  • Perennials: Coral bells, Wild geranium, Bee balm
  • Shrubs: Ninebark, Buttonbush (keep well-watered)

Avoid: Junipers, ornamental grasses near structures

Creating Wildlife Habitat

  • Food: Native plants provide berries, seeds, nectar
  • Water: Bird baths, small ponds (maintain to prevent mosquitoes)
  • Cover: Dense shrubs, brush piles at property edges
  • Nesting: Leave some dead trees (snags) if safely located

Where to Obtain Native Plants

  • Virginia Native Plant Society sales
  • Local native plant nurseries
  • Master Gardener plant sales
  • Online native plant suppliers

Always verify plants are nursery-propagated, not wild-collected!
